Backstory & Origins

Also known as the Rowans, are natives throughout the regions of Northern Hemisphere also found in the mountains of western China and the Himalayas. Rowans have bear fruits and was domesticated throughout Europe and North America.



The rowans have many names throughout different regions. Some which say it was borrowed from the old name ‘syrfe’ or ‘service tree’, old English its known as cwic-beám, in irish its known as cairtheand, in german it’s known as ‘bird-berry tree’ and Canada and Scotia it is known as the ‘dogberry tree’.

Rowans are uniquely surrounded with mythologies and folklores. In European mythology and folklore, rowans are thought to be magical tree that gave protection against malevolent beings. The tree was also called ‘wayfarer's tree’ or ‘traveler's tree’ for those who believe it prevents people from getting lost. Some British folklores are said it would repel witches. Therefore it is found planted near gates or in front of the house in Scotland. It is known that some birds who consumed the rowan’s fruits have droppings on other trees which some have successfully grown into an epiphyte. Those kind of epiphyte are known to some as ‘the flying rowan’ which some believe it acts as a counter charm against black magics, sorceries and even fairies.

In Norse mythologies, rowans are called ‘the salvation of thor’ because in mythology, Thor survived from drowning by clinging himself to a rowan branch. In Neo-Druidism, the rowan is known as ‘the portal tree’ which bridges this world with the other world. Other popular folklores are such where if there are heavy fruit bearings in a rowan, it would mean that a harsh winter is bestowing upon them that brought heavy snow. In Finland it is saying that with heavy fruit bearing comes good harvest and yield. If a rowan tree grows pale or lost some of its colour, the coming fall and winter would bring illness to the Swedish.



Fun Fact: J. R. R. Tolkien has created a character call ‘Quickbeam’ as a signature tree for the Ents in his novel ‘The two towers’.
